Full report

The full report — when you've made your shortlist

Once you've got your shortlist, that's when you need to know everything.

Not everything about the car. Everything about the situation.

The car
Has it been in a crash. Has it been written off and put back together. Is it still subject to outstanding finance — because if it is, the finance company can repossess it from you even after you've paid. Has the mileage been clocked. Has it been stolen and given a clean plate.

These are yes or no questions. They have yes or no answers.
The seller
Is this a real business or just a name on a listing. How long have they been trading. What are their customers saying — not the headline rating, the pattern underneath it. Who are the people behind it and is there anything in their history that should give you pause.

This is what everyone else misses. The car can be clean. The seller can still be the problem.
Your rights
They depend entirely on how you're buying. Collecting the car in person is different from buying it remotely. A dealer is different from a private seller. The same problem, the same car, the same outcome — but completely different rights depending on how the transaction worked.

We tell you which situation you're in and what that means. In plain English. Before anything goes wrong, while you can still do something about it.

Specific to what we found. Not a generic checklist — the actual things that matter for this car and this seller. Before you hand over any money.

What we left out

Valuations to five decimal places. Finance tables. Insurance comparisons. Tyre specifications. OE build sheets.

All of that is somewhere else on the internet for free if you want it. It isn't what you need in the moment you're deciding whether to trust someone with several thousand pounds.

We left it out on purpose.
