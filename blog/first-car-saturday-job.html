First car buying guide

Your first car — what your money actually buys

The honest guide for 2026. Real cars, real insurance groups, real road tax

You still don't think you ever want to see another carton of fries. But it's time to turn that toil and sweat into your first set of wheels.

Here's how to spend that hard-earned cash wisely.

Under £2,500

What your Saturday job actually buys you

First, the bad news. Every clean Aygo on AutoTrader is £3,200. The ones under £2,500 have been round the clock twice and have a service history that's mostly gaps. That's the car scene tax at work — even at the bottom of the market, everyone's chasing the same cars.

The good news is that the cars nobody's chasing are often better.

The cars

Skoda Citigo S or SE Greentech (2012–2016)

Insurance group 1  ·  Road tax £20/yr  ·  Budget £1,200–£2,200

Insurance group 1. Road tax £20 a year. Let that sink in.

The Citigo is the same car as the VW Up — built in the same factory, same engine, same gearbox, same reliability record. It costs less because it says Skoda on the back instead of VW. That's the entire difference. And at £1,200–£2,200 for a clean one, it is genuinely the best value first car in the UK right now.

The SE Greentech is the trim to look for. It adds air conditioning and the stop-start system that earns the £20 road tax. Avoid the basic S if it doesn't have air con.

One thing to budget for: cambelt change every three years or 30,000 miles. Check when it was last done before you hand over any money.

VW Up Move Up (2012–2015)

Insurance group 1  ·  Road tax £20/yr  ·  Budget £1,800–£2,500

Same car as the Citigo, slightly more expensive because of the badge. Insurance group 1, road tax £20 a year. If you find a clean one at £2,500 or under, buy it. If the Citigo is £300 cheaper and identical, buy the Citigo.

Ford Ka Edge (2010–2016)

Insurance group 2–4  ·  Road tax ~£30/yr  ·  Budget £1,000–£2,000

The Edge trim is the one — introduced after January 2010, it adds electric windows, remote central locking and air conditioning. The base Studio has none of those. Don't buy a Studio.

Fords are cheap to fix, parts are everywhere, and a good Ka Edge with sensible mileage and a decent history is findable at £1,000–£2,000. That's money left over for insurance. At this budget, that matters.

Nissan Micra Acenta K13 (2013–2015)

Insurance group 5–7  ·  Road tax ~£30/yr  ·  Budget £1,500–£2,500

A little higher on insurance than the Citigo and the Ka, but still well within first-car territory. The Acenta trim has air conditioning, cruise control, alloys and electric door mirrors. Don't buy a Visia — it doesn't have air con and you'll miss it.

Known issue: clutch wear on the K13. Not a dealbreaker, but get a mechanic to check it before you commit.
At this budget, the spec sheet matters less than the history. One careful owner with a full service file beats two owners with a clean MOT every time. Take your time. The right car is out there.

Under £5,000

If granny came through

If you're lucky enough to have some money that granny tucked into an ISA, you might be able to stretch to one of these. Another year of birthdays and Christmases, or a second Saturday job, and the market opens up considerably.

The cars

Hyundai i10 1.0 SE (2015–2018)

Insurance group 2  ·  Road tax £20–£200*  ·  Budget £3,500–£5,000

Insurance group 2 — lower even than the Picanto. The SE trim is where Hyundai put the useful equipment: air conditioning, cruise control, Bluetooth, DAB radio. The base S trim saves you nothing worth saving and leaves you without air con.

The 1.0 engine is the right call. The 1.2 sounds tempting but jumps straight to group 6 and above. Cheap to buy, cheap to insure, cheap to run — and the pre-2017 cars still pay around £20 a year in road tax if you find the right plate.

Plate tip: a pre-April 2017 i10 SE still pays around £20 road tax. Post-2017 goes to £200 flat. Check the plate before you buy — it could save you £180 a year.

Kia Picanto 1.0 in 2 trim (2017 onwards)

Insurance group 3  ·  Road tax £200/yr  ·  Budget £4,500–£6,000

The 2 trim is the sweet spot: air conditioning, electric windows, insurance group 3. Avoid the GT-Line — it pushes the insurance group up for no reliability gain. Avoid the automatic — more fuel, more cost, and the CVT gearbox has a known history of problems.

Kia's 7-year warranty culture filters down into the used market. Previous owners tend to look after them. Parts are cheap. And because they're not the car everyone's been told to buy, you're not competing with every other first-time buyer in your postcode.

They hold their value, which is the flip side of the reliability reputation. Budget £4,500–£6,000 for a clean one — you may need to stretch slightly above £5,000 for the best examples.

Nissan Micra Acenta K14 (2017 onwards)

Insurance group 5–8  ·  Road tax £200/yr  ·  Budget £4,000–£5,500

Better built than the K13 it replaced — the clutch issues went away with the new generation. The Acenta trim has air conditioning, cruise control and a seven-inch touchscreen. Insurance groups run 5–8, a little higher than the Korean cars, but still well within first-car territory.

The reputation as an older driver's car works entirely in your favour. Lower desirability premium, often meticulously maintained, and you're not fishing in the same pond as everyone else.

At this budget, the scene tax argument still applies. A well-kept i10 or Micra at £4,500 with two owners and a full history is a better buy than a tired Aygo at £4,500 with five owners and a thin file. Same money. Different car. You know which one to choose.

Whatever you're buying

Run the reg before you go anywhere near it. Not after — before.

The car can be completely fine. The seller can still be a problem. A private seller with no verifiable history, a phone number that doesn't match the area, and a listing that appeared yesterday — that's not a car problem. That's a seller problem.

Check both. Then go and buy your car.
